## SMVP (Share, Model, View, Psychology)

- New software architectural pattern: SMVP (Share, Model, View, Psychology). Its key distinction is aiming for a balance between inheritance and refactoring. Most popular patterns (MVC, MVP, MVVM) lean heavily toward inheritance, which often increases time spent on adding/changing features and tracking bugs. Going all‑in on refactoring is even worse.
There’s a “golden mean”: 10,000 lines of perfect code and 20,000 lines of bad code are not better than 15,000 lines of high‑quality code. Reasoning: writing 10,000 lines of perfect code takes more time than writing 15,000 lines of high‑quality code. Writing 20,000 lines of bad code is fastest initially, but long‑term maintenance becomes very hard due to missing or absent design patterns. Over time, feature work is fastest with 15,000 lines of high‑quality code, while bug hunting is fastest with 10,000 lines of perfect code. Thus, the realistic choice is between 10,000 lines of perfect code and 15,000 lines of high‑quality code.
With frequent feature additions and changes, development with 15,000 lines of high‑quality code will be fastest, because in some areas it’s quicker to edit a few lines than to create files and extend classes. However, if bugs appear often, debugging will take longer than with 10,000 lines of perfect code, where modules are easy to test thanks to comprehensive (unit and integration) coverage. Conclusion: development is faster with 15,000 lines of high‑quality code, but if teams introduce many bugs, aim for 10,000 lines of perfect code.
How to achieve this balance? By leveraging psychology (cognitive and gestalt) and design patterns—this is what ultimately leads to SMVP.

#### Gestalt psychology

- Gestalt psychology studies how we perceive holistic forms: why the brain organizes elements into structures (figure and ground), groups them by proximity/similarity, and “completes” a whole rather than perceiving a mere sum of parts.
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestalt_psychology
